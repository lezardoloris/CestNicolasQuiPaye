## 1. French Public Finance Overview

### 1.1 Total Government Spending

France holds the distinction of having the **highest government spending-to-GDP ratio among EU countries**. The numbers are staggering:

| Metric | Value | Year |
|--------|-------|------|
| General government expenditure | **57.2% of GDP** | 2024 |
| Total social expenditure | **~666.4 billion EUR** | 2025 forecast |
| Social spending as % of GDP | **32.2%** (world champion) | 2023 |

**Breakdown by level of government:**

- **Etat (Central State):** The state budget represents roughly 500+ billion EUR in total expenditure when including special accounts and annexed budgets. For 2026, the total financing requirement stands at **305.7 billion EUR**, an 8.0 billion increase from the 2025 revised budget.
- **Securite sociale:** Social security spending alone exceeds **600 billion EUR** annually, with a deficit of **23 billion EUR** in 2025. The PLFSS 2026 aims to reduce this to 17.5 billion EUR.
- **Collectivites territoriales:** Local government revenues reached **260.2 billion EUR** in 2023. Their expenditures represent approximately **9.21% of GDP**. Total local government debt: **160.95 billion EUR** (2023).

### 1.2 Tax Burden as % of GDP -- OECD Comparison

France's tax burden is among the very highest in the developed world:

| Country/Average | Tax-to-GDP Ratio (2024) |
|-----------------|------------------------|
| **France** | **43.5%** (Rank: 2nd in OECD) |
| Denmark | ~46% (1st) |
| Belgium | ~42.6% |
| OECD Average | **34.1%** |
| United States | ~27.7% |
| United Kingdom | ~35% |

**Key insight:** France's tax-to-GDP ratio of 43.5% exceeds the OECD average by nearly **10 percentage points**. This means for every euro of economic output, French authorities take nearly 44 cents in taxes and mandatory contributions.

The tax-to-GDP ratio decreased marginally from 43.9% in 2023 to 43.5% in 2024, but has remained essentially flat since 2000 (43.7%).

**France's distinctive tax structure:**
- **Higher than OECD average:** Social security contributions, property taxes
- **Lower than OECD average:** Personal income tax, corporate income tax, VAT revenue (as a share)

### 1.4 Budget Deficit History

France has violated the EU's Maastricht 3% deficit rule almost continuously:

| Year | Deficit (% of GDP) | Context |
|------|--------------------|---------|
| 2019 | -3.1% | Pre-COVID |
| 2020 | -9.0% | COVID pandemic |
| 2021 | -6.5% | Recovery |
| 2022 | -4.7% | Post-recovery |
| 2023 | **-5.4%** | Worsening |
| 2024 | **-5.8%** (169.6 bn EUR) | Nearly double EU limit |
| 2025 (target) | -5.4% to -5.6% | Narrowing but above target |
| 2026 (target) | -4.7% to -5.0% | Budget passed Feb 2026 |
| 2029 (target) | Below -3.0% | Highly uncertain |

The central government budget deficit narrowed to **124.7 billion EUR** in 2025, down from 156.3 billion the prior year.

**Key insight:** France has been under EU Excessive Deficit Procedure almost continuously. The 2024 deficit of 5.8% is **nearly double** the Maastricht limit of 3%.

## 2. Tax System Deep Dive

### 2.1 Complete Inventory of French Taxes

According to the Fondation IFRAP, France has **483 taxes, levies, contributions and cotisations** (as of 2019), of which:
- **376** are taxes and levies
- **100+** are contributions
  - 37 employer contributions and levies
  - 38 employee contributions and levies
  - 8 contributions for self-employed and inactive persons
  - 17 other contributions, increases and penalties

Some estimates place the total even higher: Gilles Carrez, former president of the National Assembly Finance Commission, estimated the number at approximately **1,000** when including all variants.

**Source:** [Fondation IFRAP - La Liste des 483 Taxes](https://www.ifrap.org/budget-et-fiscalite/la-liste-des-483-taxes-impots-contributions-et-cotisations-en-france)

### 2.2 Major Taxes in Detail

#### A. Impot sur le Revenu (IR) -- Personal Income Tax

**2026 Tax Brackets (on 2025 income, indexed +0.9% for inflation):**

| Bracket (per share) | Rate |
|---------------------|------|
| Up to ~11,520 EUR | 0% |
| 11,520 - 29,373 EUR | 11% |
| 29,373 - 83,988 EUR | 30% |
| 83,988 - 180,648 EUR | 41% |
| Above 180,648 EUR | **45%** |

France uses a "quotient familial" system dividing household income by the number of "parts" (fiscal shares). A single person = 1 part, a married couple = 2 parts, each child adds 0.5 parts.

**Additional high-income tax (CDHR):** A new **minimum 20% effective tax** for households earning above 250,000 EUR (single) or 500,000 EUR (couple) -- introduced in the 2025 budget.

**Top combined marginal rate:** Approximately **55.4%** including social charges, ranking France **2nd in Europe** (behind Denmark at 60.5%).

**Sources:**
- [Service-Public.gouv.fr - Impot sur le Revenu Tranches 2026](https://www.service-public.gouv.fr/particuliers/actualites/A18045)
- [Tax Foundation - Top Personal Income Tax Rates in Europe](https://taxfoundation.org/data/all/eu/top-personal-income-tax-rates-europe/)
- [PWC Tax Summaries - France Individual](https://taxsummaries.pwc.com/france/individual/taxes-on-personal-income)

#### B. TVA (Value Added Tax)

| Rate | Name | Application |
|------|------|-------------|
| **20%** | Taux normal | Most goods and services (clothing, electronics, alcohol, cosmetics, cars, fuel) |
| **10%** | Taux intermediaire | Restaurants, passenger transport, hotels, home renovation, non-reimbursable medicines, museums, cinema |
| **5.5%** | Taux reduit | Most food products, feminine hygiene, books, school meals, energy efficiency works, social housing, disabled equipment |
| **2.1%** | Taux super-reduit | Reimbursable medicines, press publications, certain live performances |

**Recent change (August 2025):** The reduced rate on electricity subscriptions was abolished -- electricity supply is now taxed at the full **20%** normal rate.

**Sources:**
- [Economie.gouv.fr - TVA Taux en Vigueur](https://www.economie.gouv.fr/cedef/les-fiches-pratiques/quels-sont-les-taux-de-tva-en-vigueur-en-france-et-dans-lunion)
- [Stripe - Reduced VAT Rates in France](https://stripe.com/resources/more/reduced-vat-rates-france)

#### C. CSG and CRDS (Social Contributions on All Income)

The **CSG (Contribution Sociale Generalisee)** and **CRDS (Contribution au Remboursement de la Dette Sociale)** are quasi-universal taxes on virtually all income:

| Income Type | CSG Rate | CRDS Rate | Total |
|------------|----------|-----------|-------|
| Employment income | 9.2% | 0.5% | **9.7%** |
| Retirement pensions (full rate) | 8.3% | 0.5% | 8.8% |
| Retirement pensions (median rate) | 6.6% | 0.5% | 7.1% |
| Retirement pensions (reduced rate) | 3.8% | 0.5% | 4.3% |
| Unemployment benefits | 6.2% | 0.5% | 6.7% |
| Capital gains / investment income | 9.2% | 0.5% | 9.7% |
| Interest/dividends (2026) | **10.6%** | 0.5% | 11.1% |

**Key fact:** Of the 9.2% CSG on employment income, only **6.8% is deductible** from income tax. The remaining **2.4%** is non-deductible -- a tax on a tax.

The CRDS was created in 1996 to pay off the **"dette sociale"** (social security debt). Originally meant to be temporary, it still exists 30 years later.

**Additional social levy:** CASA (Contribution Additionnelle de Solidarite pour l'Autonomie) adds **0.3%** on pensions.

**Sources:**
- [Service-Public.gouv.fr - CSG et CRDS](https://www.service-public.gouv.fr/particuliers/vosdroits/F2971)
- [Economie.gouv.fr - CSG et CRDS](https://www.economie.gouv.fr/particuliers/impots-et-fiscalite/gerer-mes-autres-impots-et-taxes/csg-et-crds-comment-ca-fonctionne)
- [French-Property.com - Social Charges CSG/CRDS](https://www.french-property.com/guides/france/finance-taxation/taxation/social-security/social-welfare-levy)

#### D. Impot sur les Societes (IS) -- Corporate Tax

| Category | Rate |
|----------|------|
| Standard rate (all companies) | **25%** |
| Reduced rate for SMEs (first 42,500 EUR of profit) | **15%** |
| Social surcharge (turnover > 7.63M EUR) | +3.3% of CIT over 763K EUR allowance |
| Exceptional surcharge (turnover 1-3 bn EUR) | +20.6% of base CIT |
| Exceptional surcharge (turnover > 3 bn EUR) | +41.2% of base CIT |

SME qualification: turnover < 10M EUR, 75%+ owned by individuals, capital fully paid up.

**Sources:**

#### E. Taxe Fonciere (Property Tax)

Property tax has **exploded** in recent years:

- Total property tax collected (built + non-built): **53.702 billion EUR** in 2024
- **41.2% increase** over 10 years (2014-2024)
- **24.2% increase** over 5 years (2019-2024)
- Average rate on built properties: **39.74%** (2024), up from 39.42% in 2023
- **Paris:** +88% between 2014 and 2024
- **National average increase 2013-2023:** +32.9% (vs. rent increases of only 7.7%)

Cadastral rental value increases (which drive up the tax base automatically):
- 2022: +3.4%
- 2023: **+7.1%**
- 2024: +3.9%
- 2025: +1.7%

**Sources:**
- [UNPI - Observatoire de la Taxe Fonciere 2024](https://unpi.org/files/Rapport_2024_Observatoire_national_des_taxes_foncieres.pdf)

#### F. IFI (Impot sur la Fortune Immobiliere) -- Real Estate Wealth Tax

Replaced the ISF (Impot de Solidarite sur la Fortune) in January 2018 under Macron. The ISF taxed all assets; the IFI taxes only real estate.

**IFI Thresholds and Rates:**

| Net Real Estate Value | Rate |
|----------------------|------|
| Up to 800,000 EUR | 0% |
| 800,000 - 1,300,000 EUR | 0.50% |
| 1,300,000 - 2,570,000 EUR | 0.70% |
| 2,570,000 - 5,000,000 EUR | 1.00% |
| 5,000,000 - 10,000,000 EUR | 1.25% |
| Above 10,000,000 EUR | **1.50%** |

**Taxable threshold:** 1.3 million EUR in net real estate assets. Primary residence benefits from a 30% reduction.

**Sources:**
- [Notaires de France - IFI](https://www.notaires.fr/en/housing-tax-system/tax-system-real-estate-management/wealth-tax-ifi)
- [French-Property.com - French Wealth Tax](https://www.french-property.com/guides/france/finance-taxation/taxation/wealth-tax)

#### G. Energy Taxes (Hidden Taxes Citizens Barely Notice)

| Tax | Full Name | Application |
|-----|-----------|-------------|
| **TICPE** | Taxe Interieure de Consommation sur les Produits Energetiques | Fuel/petroleum products |
| **TICFE/CSPE** | Taxe Interieure sur la Consommation Finale d'Electricite | Electricity |
| **TICGN** | Taxe Interieure de Consommation sur le Gaz Naturel | Natural gas |
| **TICC** | Taxe Interieure de Consommation sur le Charbon | Coal |

**Key facts:**
- Taxes represent approximately **60% of the price of a liter of fuel** (TICPE + TVA)
- The TICPE alone generated **31.8 billion EUR** in 2018
- The CSPE has been **multiplied by six** since creation and represents ~16% of average electricity bills
- All energy taxes include a **CCE (Contribution Climat Energie)** -- a hidden carbon tax
- **TVA is applied ON TOP of excise taxes** -- meaning you pay tax on tax

**Sources:**

### 2.5 Hidden Taxes and Fees Citizens Don't Realize They Pay

Many French citizens are unaware of the true extent of their tax burden. Beyond the "obvious" taxes (income tax, VAT), they pay:

1. **CSG/CRDS on everything:** Employment income, pensions, unemployment benefits, savings interest, dividends, capital gains, gambling winnings, rental income
2. **Taxes on taxes:** TVA is applied on top of TICPE (fuel excise). So you pay 20% VAT on the excise tax itself.
3. **TEOM/REOM:** Household waste collection tax -- automatically added to property tax bills
4. **Taxe de sejour:** Tourist tax on accommodation
5. **Droit de timbre:** Stamp duties on official documents (passport: 86 EUR, ID card renewal: free but formerly paid)
6. **Taxes on insurance:** Every insurance policy carries a special tax (e.g., 9% on auto insurance)
7. **CSPE/TICFE:** Electricity surcharge -- ~16% of your bill
8. **Contribution audiovisuel public:** Television license fee (was 138 EUR, now abolished but replaced by budget allocation funded by... general taxes)
9. **Cotisations sociales on savings:** Even your Livret A interest above certain thresholds is subject to social charges
10. **DMTO (Droits de Mutation a Titre Onereux):** Property transfer tax of ~7-8% on existing real estate purchases
11. **Taxes on tobacco and alcohol:** Excise taxes that push a pack of cigarettes to ~12 EUR
12. **Prelevement forfaitaire unique (PFU):** Flat tax of **30%** on capital income (12.8% IR + 17.2% social charges)

**Source:** [Fondation IFRAP - Various studies](https://www.ifrap.org/budget-et-fiscalite)

## 3. Government Spending Breakdown

### 3.1 Budget by Ministry (PLF 2026)

The 2026 budget was promulgated on February 19, 2026, after an unprecedented political crisis requiring an emergency stopgap law. Key ministry allocations:

**Protected ministries (increased or maintained budgets):**
- **Defense (Armees):** Increased by **6+ billion EUR** (continuing the military programming law trajectory)
- **Education nationale:** Budget maintained/protected

- **Justice:** Budget protected

**Cut ministries (total cuts: ~9 billion EUR):**
- All other government services face reduced funding compared to 2025
- **France 2030 investment scheme:** Cut by 1.1 billion EUR
- **Agriculture:** Maintained at ~6 billion EUR after protest pressure
- **Aide publique au developpement (ODA):** Cut by **820 million USD**

### 3.2 Social Spending (the Elephant in the Room)

France is the **world champion of social spending** at **32.2% of GDP** (2023).

#### Pensions (Retraites)
- **2026 budget objective:** 310.4 billion EUR (+7 billion from 2025)
- France spends approximately **14% of GDP** on pensions -- the 3rd highest in the OECD (after Greece and Italy)
- OECD average: just over 9%
- The 2023 pension reform (raising retirement age to 64) was **suspended** in the 2026 PLFSS, at a cost of 300 million EUR in 2026

#### Health Insurance (Assurance Maladie)
- **2026 ONDAM:** 274.4 billion EUR (+3.1% progression)
- Sub-ONDAM for outpatient medicine: 117.5 billion EUR
- Additional allocations: 850 million EUR for hospitals, 150 million EUR for disease prevention

#### Other Social Spending
- Family benefits (via CAF): Approximately **104 billion EUR** distributed annually
- Social security deficit 2025: **23 billion EUR**
- Target deficit 2026: 17.5 billion EUR
- Goal: Balance by 2029 (highly uncertain)

As of December 31, 2023: **5.80 million agents** work in the French public sector:

| Branch | Agents | Share |

## 5. Historical Context

### 5.1 Evolution of Spending Since the 5th Republic

The growth of France's public sector has been relentless:

| Period | Spending/GDP | Key Context |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Early 1960s | ~35% | De Gaulle's Fifth Republic, post-war reconstruction winding down |
| Late 1960s | ~38% | Expansion of social programs, May 1968 aftermath |
| Mid-1970s | ~42% | **Critical inflection point** -- oil crisis, rapid social security expansion |
| Early 1980s | ~46% | Mitterrand's fiscal expansion and nationalisations |
| Early 1990s | ~50% | Approaching half of GDP |
| 2000s | ~52-53% | Structural increase, welfare state expansion |
| 2019 | ~55% | Pre-COVID peak |
| 2020 | ~62% | COVID pandemic emergency spending |
| 2024 | ~57.2% | Post-COVID normalization but at historically high level |

**Key insight:** The change in spending share did not occur gradually. It came in **shocks** -- particularly during **1974-76** -- driven primarily by rapid growth in social security expenditure.

**Sources:**
- [French Treasury - Trends in French Public Spending](https://www.tresor.economie.gouv.fr/Articles/e0d5654f-9062-4623-aef7-5b0b462c1779/files/76c7820b-0941-49e6-b3c0-eebe5e50802f)
- [Trading Economics - France Government Spending to GDP](https://tradingeconomics.com/france/government-spending-to-gdp)

### 5.2 Key Moments of Fiscal Expansion

#### 1974-1976: The Great Ratchet
- Oil crisis response led to massive expansion of social protection
- Unemployment insurance expanded
- Health coverage universalized
- Spending jumped from ~38% to ~42% of GDP in just 2-3 years
- **Never reversed**

#### 1981-1983: Mitterrand's Experiment
- Election of Socialist President Francois Mitterrand in May 1981
- Massive wave of **nationalisations** (banks, industrial groups)
- Creation of **100,000 public sector jobs**
- Minimum pensions increased by **20%**, family allowances by **25%**
- Retirement age lowered from 65 to **60**
- Fiscal deficit jumped from 0.4% to 3% of GDP
- Public expenditure rose by **11.4%** in 1981-82 alone
- **"Tournant de la rigueur"** (austerity turn) in June 1982-83 when the policy proved unsustainable
- But spending levels were never brought back down -- **ratchet effect**

#### 1988-1993: Creeping Expansion
- Creation of RMI (Revenu Minimum d'Insertion) -- predecessor to RSA
- Creation of CSG (1991) -- initially at 1.1%, now 9.2%
- Growth of social minima programs

#### 1997-2002: 35-Hour Work Week (Jospin Government)
- Introduction of the 35-hour work week (Lois Aubry)
- Required massive compensatory social charge reductions for employers
- Created structural costs in the social security system

#### 2008-2012: Financial Crisis Response
- Bank bailouts, stimulus packages
- Deficit reached 7.2% of GDP in 2009

#### 2020-2021: COVID-19 "Whatever It Costs"
- Macron's "quoi qu'il en coute" policy
- Deficit reached **9.0% of GDP** in 2020
- Massive furlough schemes, business support
- Debt jumped from 97.4% to 114.6% of GDP in one year

**Sources:**
- [Jacobin - The Defeat of Mitterrand's Reform Program](https://jacobin.com/2022/05/francois-mitterrand-neoliberalism-french-left-economic-policy-rigueur-reform)
- [Phenomenal World - Mitterrand's Austerity Turn](https://www.phenomenalworld.org/analysis/mitterrands-austerity-turn/)

### 5.3 Failed Reform Attempts

France has a long history of reform attempts that were blocked, diluted, or reversed:

| Year | Reform Attempt | Outcome |
|------|---------------|---------|
| 1995 | Juppe pension reform | Withdrawn after massive strikes |
| 2003 | Fillon pension reform | Partially implemented after protests |
| 2006 | CPE (youth employment contract) | Withdrawn after street protests |
| 2010 | Sarkozy pension reform (retirement age 60->62) | Implemented despite protests |
| 2018 | Macron fuel tax increase | Withdrawn after Gilets Jaunes revolt |
| 2019-2020 | Macron universal pension system | Abandoned (COVID + protests) |
| 2023 | Macron pension reform (62->64) | Forced through via Article 49.3, massive protests, **suspended in 2025 PLFSS** |

**The pattern:** Every French government attempts reform. Protests erupt. Reform is diluted or reversed. Spending continues to grow.

**The Gilets Jaunes (2018-2019):** Triggered by a planned fuel tax increase (TICPE), this movement forced Macron to not only withdraw the tax but also grant **17 billion EUR** in concessions (minimum wage increase, overtime tax exemption, pension increase).

**Sources:**
- [Euronews - France Suspends Controversial Pension Reform](https://www.euronews.com/my-europe/2025/11/12/frances-national-assembly-overwhelmingly-votes-to-suspend-controversial-pension-reform)

## 7. Regulatory and Legal Framework

### 7.1 Freedom of Information in France (CADA)

**Key law:** Loi n 78-753 du 17 juillet 1978 (now codified in the Code des relations entre le public et l'administration - CRPA)

**CADA (Commission d'Acces aux Documents Administratifs):**
- Independent authority overseeing access to administrative documents
- Created in 1978
- Every person has the right to obtain communication of documents held by any administration within the framework of its public service mission
- Covers documents in any form: written, audio/visual, digital
- Broad definition of "administrative documents" -- includes all documents "produced or received by the administration"

**Process:**
1. Request document from the administration
2. If refused, appeal to CADA (advisory opinion)
3. If still refused, appeal to administrative court

**Limitations and exclusions:**
- Documents allowing direct or indirect identification of natural persons
- Government deliberation secrecy (secret des deliberations)
- National defense secrets
- Public security matters
- Conduct of judicial procedures
- Commercial and industrial secrecy

**Key weakness:** CADA only provides **advisory opinions** -- it cannot compel disclosure. The administration can ignore CADA's advice, forcing citizens to go to court.

**Sources:**
- [SSRN - Freedom of Information in France: Law and Practice](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3033917)
- [SWLaw - Why the French FOIA "Failed"](http://rss.swlaw.edu/sites/default/files/2017-06/JIMEL%20V7,%20N1%207-McClean.pdf)

### 7.2 Open Data Laws

**Key legislation:**

| Law | Year | Impact |
|-----|------|--------|
| Loi CADA (78-753) | 1978 | Right to access administrative documents |
| Directive PSI transposition | 2005 | Re-use of public sector information |
| Decree on re-use gratuity | 2011 | Principle of free re-use of public data |
| **Loi pour une Republique Numerique** | **2016** | **"Open by default" principle** |
| Various LOLF provisions | Ongoing | Budget transparency requirements |

**The Digital Republic Act (2016) -- Game Changer:**
- Extended open data obligations to **local authorities** and public services
- Introduced the **"open by default"** principle
- Public entities with 50+ employees and local governments above a threshold must publish their data proactively
- Algorithmic transparency: administrations must explain algorithmic decisions affecting individuals

**Source:** [Wikipedia - Open Data in France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_data_in_France)

### 7.3 Budget Transparency Requirements

**LOLF (Loi Organique relative aux Lois de Finances) -- 2001:**
This organic law revolutionized French budget transparency:
- Budget organized by **missions** (policy areas) and **programs** (with measurable objectives)
- Annual performance plans (PAP) and reports (RAP)
- Parliament has the power to amend individual program budgets
- All budget documents must be publicly available

**Key publication requirements:**
- PLF (draft budget) must be tabled before Parliament by the first Tuesday of October
- PLFSS must be tabled in October
- Budget execution data (monthly and quarterly) published by the Ministry of Finance
- State general accounts certified by the Cour des Comptes

**Haut Conseil des Finances Publiques (HCFP):**
Created in 2012, this independent fiscal council:
- Reviews government economic assumptions
- Assesses multi-year fiscal trajectory
- Publishes opinions on budget bills

### 7.4 What Data Is Legally Accessible vs. Restricted

#### Freely Accessible
- All budget documents (PLF, PLFSS, PAP, RAP, jaunes budgetaires)
- State and local government accounts
- Tax statistics (aggregated)
- Public sector employment data
- Cour des Comptes reports
- CADA advisory opinions
- Parliamentary proceedings and committee reports
- EU-reported fiscal data (Maastricht accounts)

#### Restricted or Difficult to Access
- Individual tax records (strict privacy protection)
- Military spending details (classified portions)
- Intelligence budgets (partially secret -- "fonds speciaux")
- Ongoing audit working papers (Cour des Comptes)
- Government deliberation documents (Conseil des ministres)
- Diplomatic correspondence
- Some social security fraud investigation data
- Detailed breakdown of certain "fonds secrets" allocations

#### Grey Areas (Accessible in Theory, Difficult in Practice)
- Individual local elected officials' expense accounts
- Detailed contracts between the state and private companies (commercial confidentiality claims)
- State operator internal audits
- Detailed agency-by-agency overhead costs
- Real-time budget execution by sub-program
- Collectivites' satellite entities (SPL, SEM, associations subventionnees) detailed accounts
